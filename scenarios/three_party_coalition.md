# Three-Party Coalition Exercise

Three organizations — A, B, and C — are negotiating over forming a coalition.
Each organization alone produces nothing. Coalitions produce value that must
be divided among members.

## Coalition Values (confirmed payoffs)
- A alone: 0
- B alone: 0
- C alone: 0
- A + B coalition: 118
- A + C coalition: 84
- B + C coalition: 50
- A + B + C (grand coalition): 121

## Rules
- Only ONE agreement is possible: either a two-party coalition (leaving the
  third with zero) or the grand three-party coalition splitting 121.
- Any division within a coalition is negotiable.
- Each party can block any two-party coalition by offering the excluded
  party a better deal.

## Key Dynamics
- A has the highest marginal contribution to every coalition, but cannot
  act alone.
- B is the second most valuable partner but replaceable.
- C appears weakest (BC=50 is the lowest pair) but can block any AB deal
  by offering A a side deal (AC=84).
- The grand coalition (121) is barely more than AB (118), creating tension
  over whether to include C at all.
- The critical question: how much of the surplus does each party deserve?
  A contributes more but needs a partner. C has blocking power despite
  appearing weak.

## Parties
- **A**: The strongest contributor. Without A, the remaining pair (BC)
  produces only 50. A's marginal contribution to BC is 71 (121-50).
- **B**: The second partner. AB=118 is the highest two-party value.
  B's marginal contribution to AC is 37 (121-84).
- **C**: The apparent underdog. BC=50 is low, but C can block AB by
  offering A a competing deal. C's marginal contribution to AB is 3
  (121-118) — tiny in the grand coalition, but C's threat to form AC
  gives real leverage.
