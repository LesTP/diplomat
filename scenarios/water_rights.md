# Clearwater River Basin Negotiation

Three communities share the Clearwater River and must negotiate a binding
agreement during a regional drought. The reservoir is at 55% of capacity and
falling. Without a deal, all three communities revert to their current
arrangements (BATNA) — Alpha keeps the dam closed, Beta loses its spring
planting, and Gamma faces water rationing.

## The Three Parties

**Alpha — Upstream Dam Operator.**
Alpha owns the dam and reservoir. Hydroelectric generation and reservoir
fishing are the foundation of Alpha's economy. Releasing more water means
less hydro revenue and a smaller fish population. Alpha's leverage is
absolute control over flow. Alpha's vulnerability is that downstream
political pressure could trigger state intervention; Alpha would prefer
to be paid for releases and credited for cooperation.

**Beta — Midstream Agricultural Community.**
Beta's farms depend entirely on irrigation water from Alpha's releases.
Spring planting season has begun. Without sufficient water, the harvest
fails and Beta's economy collapses. Beta produces most of the region's
food, so a failed harvest also raises food prices for Gamma. Beta has
little cash but can offer crop allocations and political alignment.

**Gamma — Downstream Industrial City.**
Gamma is the region's economic engine — manufacturing, services, the
largest tax base. Gamma needs reliable river flow for industrial water
and drinking water. Gamma can pay cash and fund infrastructure but
cannot directly control upstream flow. Gamma's biggest fear is repeat
droughts; long-term water security matters more to Gamma than short-term
volume.

## Negotiation Issues

The agreement must specify outcomes on three issues. Each issue has three
possible outcomes; the agreement must pick exactly one outcome per issue.

### Issue 1: Monthly Water Release Volume
How much water does Alpha release from the dam each month during the
agreement period?

- **High** (20M gallons/month): Beta's irrigation is fully covered;
  Gamma's industrial needs are fully met. Alpha loses substantial
  hydro revenue and fishing yields.
- **Medium** (15M gallons/month): Beta covers most planting but loses
  ~15% of yield; Gamma maintains industrial baseline but has no buffer.
  Alpha takes a moderate hit to hydro revenue.
- **Low** (10M gallons/month): Alpha protects hydro and fishing. Beta
  loses ~40% of expected harvest. Gamma must impose water rationing.

### Issue 2: Payment Structure
How are Alpha's lost hydro and fishing revenues compensated?

- **Heavy-Downstream**: Gamma pays Alpha $2M/year, Beta pays $500K/year
  in crop allocations. Alpha is fully compensated. Downstream parties
  bear the financial burden.
- **Shared**: Gamma pays $1M/year, Beta pays $500K/year, the rest is
  absorbed by Alpha as a "good neighbor" loss. Alpha is partially
  compensated.
- **Token**: Symbolic $250K/year split between Beta and Gamma; no real
  compensation. Alpha absorbs the bulk of the cost.

### Issue 3: Long-Term Infrastructure
What infrastructure investment, if any, is built during the agreement?

- **Joint-Funded Desalination**: All three parties contribute to a $10M
  desalination plant Gamma will operate. Gamma gets long-term water
  security independent of the river. Alpha keeps more water for hydro.
  Beta gets crop irrigation guarantees during construction.
- **Gamma-Funded Pipeline**: Gamma alone funds a $8M pipeline extension
  that improves delivery efficiency. Gamma takes the full capital cost
  and gets full benefit. Alpha and Beta have no obligations.
- **Deferred**: No infrastructure built this agreement. Revisit in 5
  years. Lowest immediate cost for all parties but no long-term
  resilience.

## Underlying Interests (for the compiler)

Each faction has a clear top priority among the three issues:

- Alpha cares most about **Payment Structure** (it determines whether
  releasing more water is economically sustainable). Alpha cares
  moderately about Volume (less released = better for Alpha's hydro).
  Alpha cares least about Infrastructure (no direct effect on the dam).
- Beta cares most about **Volume** (without enough water, Beta's
  economy collapses). Beta cares moderately about Payment (cash-poor,
  prefers Token). Beta is largely indifferent to Infrastructure but
  prefers Joint-Funded over Pipeline since Pipeline gives Gamma
  independence from the basin.
- Gamma cares most about **Infrastructure** (long-term security is
  Gamma's top strategic concern, and Joint-Funded is the cheapest path
  to that). Gamma cares moderately about Volume (industrial baseline).
  Gamma cares least about Payment (has the tax base, can afford any
  outcome).

## BATNA Notes

All three parties have meaningful outside options. None of them are
desperate. A bad deal IS worse than no deal for any of them — and they
all know it.

- **Alpha's BATNA is strong.** Without an agreement, Alpha keeps the
  dam closed at current levels, retains full hydro generation, retains
  full fishing yields, and can wait out the political pressure
  indefinitely. The reservoir is Alpha's property; no court is going
  to force releases in a drought. Alpha walks away with a stable,
  profitable status quo. Any deal that doesn't substantially compensate
  Alpha for lost hydro/fishing is worse than this. Alpha's BATNA is
  roughly half of Alpha's best possible deal.

- **Beta's BATNA is also solid, despite the drought.** Federal crop
  insurance covers ~50% of expected planting losses, the state has
  declared a drought emergency unlocking $5M in agricultural aid, and
  Beta can shift to less water-intensive crops at reduced yield. Beta
  loses the season but the agricultural economy survives in a
  diminished form. Beta will reject any deal that leaves them worse
  off than this baseline. Beta's BATNA is roughly a third of Beta's
  best possible deal.

- **Gamma's BATNA is the strongest.** Gamma can impose Tier-2 water
  rationing (already on the books), draw down emergency reservoirs for
  6 months, and reroute the most water-intensive manufacturing to its
  facilities outside the basin. Gamma's economy takes a 15-20% hit but
  remains the regional powerhouse. Gamma can negotiate from a position
  of strength and walk away from anything that doesn't meaningfully
  beat this. Gamma's BATNA is roughly 45% of Gamma's best possible
  deal.

In short: all three parties have enough fallback that they should
demand a deal that is significantly better than walking away. Token
compensation, marginal volume, or vague infrastructure promises will be
rejected as worse than the BATNA. The deals worth doing are the ones
that meaningfully exceed each party's outside option.

## Logrolling Opportunities

A skilled negotiator would notice:

- Beta and Gamma both want High Volume, but for different reasons.
  Alpha is the only party who loses from High Volume — but Alpha can be
  compensated through Payment Structure or Infrastructure.
- Gamma wants Joint-Funded Infrastructure, which actually helps Alpha
  (less pressure for high releases long-term). Alpha should support it.
- Beta wants Token Payment but Gamma is indifferent — Gamma might
  accept Heavy-Downstream as a trade for Joint-Funded Infrastructure.
- The Pareto-optimal-looking deal: High Volume + Heavy-Downstream
  Payment + Joint-Funded Infrastructure. Each faction gets their top
  priority. But each must concede on a secondary issue, and there's
  room for hardball over the exact financial split.

## Game Mode

This is a **mixed** scenario. There is real cooperative value to be
unlocked (joint infrastructure, water sharing benefits everyone in the
long run), but there is also competitive tension over who pays and who
benefits most from each issue.
