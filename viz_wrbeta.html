Clearwater River Basin Negotiation

Three communities share the Clearwater River and must negotiate a binding agreement during a regional drought. The reservoir is at 55% of capacity and falling. Without a deal, all three communities revert to their current arrangements (BATNA) — Alpha keeps the dam closed, Beta loses its spring planting, and Gamma faces water rationing.

Issues negotiated
water release volume — Monthly water release volume from Alpha's dam during the agreement period.
payment structure — Compensation scheme for Alpha's lost hydro and fishing revenues.
long term infrastructure — Long-term infrastructure investment and funding during the agreement.

The Game

Four rounds. Each round every faction posts one public proposal (sealed-bid — no within-round back-and-forth). After the final round the proposals are scored: a deal is reached only if all factions agree on the same outcome for every issue; otherwise each faction falls back to its BATNA.

The Three Parties

Alpha — Upstream Dam Operator. Alpha owns the dam and reservoir. Hydroelectric generation and reservoir fishing are the foundation of Alpha's economy. Releasing more water means less hydro revenue and a smaller fish population. Alpha's leverage is absolute control over flow. Alpha's vulnerability is that downstream political pressure could trigger state intervention; Alpha would prefer to be paid for releases and credited for cooperation.

Beta — Midstream Agricultural Community. Beta's farms depend entirely on irrigation water from Alpha's releases. Spring planting season has begun. Without sufficient water, the harvest fails and Beta's economy collapses. Beta produces most of the region's food, so a failed harvest also raises food prices for Gamma. Beta has little cash but can offer crop allocations and political alignment.

Gamma — Downstream Industrial City. Gamma is the region's economic engine — manufacturing, services, the largest tax base. Gamma needs reliable river flow for industrial water and drinking water. Gamma can pay cash and fund infrastructure but cannot directly control upstream flow. Gamma's biggest fear is repeat droughts; long-term water security matters more to Gamma than short-term volume.
