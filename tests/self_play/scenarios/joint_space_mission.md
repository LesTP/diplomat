# Joint Space Mission

Three space agencies must negotiate a binding agreement on a single
flagship deep-space mission. The mission will launch in 2030, but
the design partnership must be locked now — funding cycles, hardware
procurement, and crew selection all hinge on this agreement.

Without a deal, each agency falls back to its current standalone
program (BATNA). None of them can fly this mission alone; the cost
and risk exceed any single budget. But all three would prefer their
own framing of the mission, their own lead role, and the funding
split that protects their treasury.

## The Three Parties

**Alpha — National Exploration Agency.**
Government-funded, with decades of human-spaceflight heritage and a
political mandate to return crewed missions to the Moon and Mars.
Alpha's reputation depends on visible exploration milestones and
crewed firsts. Alpha's budget is large but slow-moving and subject
to congressional cycles. Alpha will accept partnering only if the
mission's headline framing matches its exploration mandate and the
funding mix doesn't expose taxpayers to commercial cost overruns.

**Beta — Multinational Science Consortium.**
A coalition of smaller national space agencies pooling resources for
science-priority missions: planetary science, Earth observation,
astrophysics. Beta's budget is the smallest and least flexible.
Beta has strong scientific instrumentation expertise but limited
hardware-integration capability. Beta needs the mission to deliver
peer-reviewed science to justify its consortium's continued funding
and will hold firm against any framing that subordinates science to
exploration spectacle or commercial demos.

**Gamma — Commercial Spaceflight Provider.**
A private company with the largest funding capacity, the fastest
hardware-build cycle in the industry, and a hunger for
commercial-tech-demonstration opportunities that prove new
capabilities to future customers. Gamma's leverage is money and
speed; its vulnerability is reputational — a single high-profile
failure damages its commercial-contract pipeline. Gamma is willing
to fund disproportionately if the mission demonstrates technology
it can commercialize after.

## The Three Issues

1. **Mission Objective.** What kind of mission this will be.
   Options: Science-Priority, Exploration-Priority, Commercial-Tech-Demo.
2. **Hardware Lead.** Which agency designs and integrates the primary
   spacecraft. Options: Alpha-Lead, Joint-Build, Gamma-Lead.
3. **Funding Split.** How the mission's total cost is divided across the
   three agencies. Options: Equal-Thirds, Commercial-Heavy (gamma covers
   50%+), Government-Heavy (alpha+beta cover 80%+).

## The Game

Four rounds. Each round, every agency makes a public proposal in
the joint negotiation channel. At the end of round four, all three
final-round proposals are evaluated. A deal is reached only if all
three agencies converge on the same outcome for every issue. If any
issue lacks unanimous agreement, the negotiation fails and each
agency falls back to its BATNA — their standalone program — which
nobody actually wants.
